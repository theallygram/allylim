Structuring Technical Bulletins for Web Publication

A guide to transforming dense regulatory text into scannable, web-ready content.

The Problem

Technical bulletins in regulated environments are often written for print distribution. When published to the web without restructuring, they create long, undifferentiated walls of text that readers cannot scan, search, or act on efficiently.

The goal: make the same information findable, scannable, and actionable on screen without altering its meaning or regulatory status.

Principles

  • One idea per paragraph. Break compound paragraphs into discrete, addressable units.
  • Front-load the action. Lead with what the reader needs to do, not the background.
  • Use heading hierarchy for navigation. H2 for major sections, H3 for sub-topics. Never skip levels.
  • Tables over lists for structured data. If information has two or more parallel attributes, use a table.
  • Link, don't repeat. Cross-reference related documents instead of duplicating content.

Before and After

An example from a spectrum licensing bulletin:

Before

Licensees are hereby advised that in accordance with the provisions set out in the framework for spectrum licensing in the 3800 MHz band as described in the decision document referenced above, the Minister has determined that all existing licensees whose licence areas overlap with the geographic service areas defined in Annex B must submit a transition plan no later than 120 days following the publication of this bulletin, and failure to submit such a plan may result in the application of the conditions set out in Section 7.2 of the licence conditions.

After

What you need to do

Submit a transition plan within 120 days of this bulletin's publication date.

Who this applies to

Licensees in the 3800 MHz band whose licence areas overlap with the geographic service areas in Annex B.

What happens if you don't

The conditions in Section 7.2 of your licence may apply. See the framework decision document for details.

Key Techniques

Scannable structure

Use descriptive headings that answer the reader's question. "What you need to do" is better than "Action Required" because it matches how people actually scan.

Front-loaded sentences

Start with the verb or the key fact. "Submit a transition plan within 120 days" puts the action and deadline first. The reader gets the answer before the context.

Chunked information

Each heading covers one aspect: what, who, consequence. A reader looking for the deadline doesn't need to parse eligibility criteria to find it.
